# Capital Adequacy Requirements (CAR) Guideline — 2026

**Issuing Authority:** Office of the Superintendent of Financial Institutions Canada (OSFI)
**Effective Date:** November 1, 2025 (applicable for fiscal years commencing in 2026)
**Replaces:** CAR Guideline — 2025 Edition
**Legislative Authority:** Bank Act, S.C. 1991, c. 46; Trust and Loan Companies Act, S.C. 1991, c. 45; Cooperative Credit Associations Act, S.C. 1991, c. 48
**BCBS Reference:** Basel III: A Global Regulatory Framework for More Resilient Banks and Banking Systems (BCBS, December 2010, revised June 2011); Basel III: Finalising Post-Crisis Reforms (BCBS, December 2017)

---

## 1. Purpose and Scope

### 1.1 Purpose

The Capital Adequacy Requirements (CAR) Guideline establishes OSFI's expectations for the measurement, management, and maintenance of regulatory capital adequacy by federally regulated deposit-taking institutions (DTIs) operating in Canada. The primary objectives of this guideline are to:

1. Ensure that institutions hold sufficient capital to absorb unexpected losses arising from credit, market, operational, and other material risks on both a going-concern and gone-concern basis;
2. Protect depositors, creditors, and the broader Canadian financial system from the consequences of institutional distress or failure;
3. Implement the internationally agreed Basel III capital framework as developed by the Basel Committee on Banking Supervision (BCBS), with domestic adjustments that reflect OSFI's supervisory judgment and the unique features of the Canadian financial system;
4. Provide a consistent and transparent basis for regulatory capital assessment across all federally regulated DTIs.

This guideline is a legally binding regulatory instrument issued by OSFI pursuant to its authority to issue guidelines under applicable legislation. Institutions are expected to comply with both the letter and spirit of these requirements. Capital ratios should be maintained comfortably above regulatory minimums at all times, and institutions must not allow their capital position to deteriorate to a point where compliance with minimums is uncertain.

### 1.2 Scope of Application

This guideline applies to all federally regulated deposit-taking institutions, including:
- Domestic banks chartered under Schedule I of the Bank Act
- Foreign bank subsidiaries chartered under Schedule II of the Bank Act
- Foreign bank branches operating under Schedule III of the Bank Act (subject to modified requirements)
- Trust and loan companies incorporated under the Trust and Loan Companies Act
- Cooperative retail associations regulated under the Cooperative Credit Associations Act

Capital adequacy requirements are applied on a **fully consolidated basis** as the primary supervisory measure, incorporating all banking subsidiaries and material off-balance sheet structures. Solo-entity capital reporting may be required at the discretion of OSFI where subsidiary capital positions are material to the soundness of the consolidated group.

Foreign bank branches are not subject to the consolidated capital ratio requirements but are instead subject to the asset-to-capital multiple and branch capital requirements set out in the Foreign Bank Branch Capital Adequacy (FBCA) Guideline.

## 2. Key Definitions

The following definitions are foundational to the application of the CAR Guideline. All definitions are consistent with BCBS standards unless an explicit domestic modification is noted.

### 2.1 Common Equity Tier 1 (CET1) Capital

The highest quality component of regulatory capital, representing the primary loss-absorbing layer of the capital structure. CET1 comprises:
- Common shares and related contributed surplus
- Retained earnings (including audited interim profits)
- Accumulated other comprehensive income (AOCI), subject to regulatory filters
- Common shares issued by consolidated subsidiaries and held by third parties (minority interest), to the extent eligible

CET1 is subject to a comprehensive set of regulatory deductions and adjustments, including: goodwill and other intangible assets (net of related deferred tax liabilities), deferred tax assets relying on future profitability, investments in own CET1 instruments, reciprocal cross-holdings in CET1 instruments of financial entities, non-significant and significant investments in the capital of other financial entities, and defined benefit pension fund deficits.

### 2.2 Additional Tier 1 (AT1) Capital

Capital instruments that are subordinated, fully paid-up, and perpetual (with no fixed maturity and no contractual or embedded incentives to redeem). AT1 instruments absorb losses on a going-concern basis, typically through contractual write-down or conversion to common equity upon a trigger event. All AT1 instruments issued by Canadian DTIs must include Non-Viability Contingent Capital (NVCC) provisions, requiring automatic conversion to common equity or write-down to zero upon a non-viability determination by OSFI.

### 2.3 Tier 2 Capital

Instruments that provide supplementary loss-absorbing capacity primarily in a gone-concern scenario. Tier 2 instruments include subordinated debt with an original maturity of at least five years and NVCC-eligible instruments that meet Tier 2 criteria. General allowances for credit losses are eligible as Tier 2 capital up to a maximum of 1.25% of credit risk-weighted assets under the Standardized Approach.

### 2.4 Total Regulatory Capital

The aggregate of CET1 capital, Additional Tier 1 capital, and Tier 2 capital. Total regulatory capital is expressed as a ratio to risk-weighted assets (RWA) to derive the Total Capital Ratio.

### 2.5 Risk-Weighted Assets (RWA)

The denominator of all risk-based capital ratios. Calculated as the sum of:
- **Credit Risk RWA:** On- and off-balance-sheet exposures multiplied by risk weights reflecting the credit quality of counterparties and the nature of the exposure, calculated under the Standardized Approach or Internal Ratings-Based (IRB) Approach.
- **Market Risk RWA:** Trading book and certain banking book positions measured under the Fundamental Review of the Trading Book Standardized Approach (FRTB-SA) or Internal Models Approach (FRTB-IMA), expressed as RWA equivalents (capital requirement × 12.5).
- **Operational Risk RWA:** Calculated under the Standardized Measurement Approach (SMA) using the Business Indicator Component, expressed as RWA equivalents (capital requirement × 12.5).

### 2.6 Leverage Ratio

A non-risk-based supplementary capital measure calculated as Tier 1 capital divided by the total leverage ratio exposure measure. The exposure measure encompasses on-balance sheet assets (net of specific deductions aligned with Tier 1 capital deductions), derivatives exposure calculated on a replacement cost plus add-on basis, securities financing transaction (SFT) exposures, and off-balance sheet items (commitments, guarantees, trade finance instruments) converted using specified credit conversion factors. The leverage ratio acts as a backstop to risk-based capital requirements, constraining excess leverage that may not be fully captured by RWA.

### 2.7 Total Loss-Absorbing Capacity (TLAC)

Applicable to domestic systemically important banks (D-SIBs) designated by OSFI. TLAC represents the minimum aggregate stock of instruments and liabilities that can absorb losses and recapitalize a failing institution during resolution without recourse to public funds. TLAC eligibility requirements and minimum ratios are set out in the standalone TLAC Guideline. TLAC requirements are complementary to but additive to the capital buffer requirements in this guideline.

### 2.8 Output Floor

A constraint on RWA calculated using OSFI-approved internal models. Effective January 1, 2026, the output floor is set at its final calibration of **72.5%**. Institutions using IRB or IMA approaches may not report RWA below 72.5% of the RWA that would result from applying the relevant Standardized Approaches across credit risk, market risk, and CVA risk. This floor prevents institutions from deriving undue capital relief from internal model optimization and ensures a minimum level of comparability across institutions.

## 3. Minimum Capital Ratios

### 3.1 Pillar 1 Minimum Requirements

Institutions must maintain the following minimum capital ratios at all times, measured on a consolidated basis:

| Capital Measure | Minimum Ratio |
|---|---|
| Common Equity Tier 1 (CET1) Ratio | 4.5% of RWA |
| Tier 1 Capital Ratio | 6.0% of RWA |
| Total Capital Ratio | 8.0% of RWA |
| Leverage Ratio | 3.0% of Leverage Ratio Exposure |

These minimums represent absolute regulatory floors. A breach of any minimum constitutes a supervisory trigger event requiring immediate OSFI engagement and may result in restrictions on capital distributions, mandatory capital-raising actions, or other supervisory measures available under applicable legislation.

### 3.2 Capital Conservation Buffer (CCB)

In addition to Pillar 1 minimums, institutions must maintain a Capital Conservation Buffer of **2.5% of RWA**, composed entirely of CET1 capital. The CCB is designed to accumulate during benign economic conditions so it may be drawn upon during stress without breaching Pillar 1 minimums. When CET1 capital falls within the buffer range (4.5% to 7.0%), institutions face graduated restrictions on discretionary distributions — including common and preferred dividends, share buybacks, and discretionary staff bonus payments — as set out in the distribution constraint framework.

### 3.3 D-SIB Surcharge

D-SIBs designated by OSFI must hold an additional CET1 surcharge of **1.0% of RWA**, reflecting the heightened systemic impact of their potential failure. The D-SIB surcharge operates in the same manner as the CCB, adding to the distribution constraint threshold. The combined CET1 minimum (Pillar 1 + CCB + D-SIB surcharge) for D-SIBs is **8.0%**, prior to application of the Domestic Stability Buffer.

### 3.4 D-SIB Leverage Ratio

D-SIBs face an enhanced minimum leverage ratio of **3.5%**, reflecting their systemic importance and OSFI's domestic calibration choice.

## 4. Domestic Stability Buffer (DSB)

### 4.1 Overview and Policy Rationale

The Domestic Stability Buffer (DSB) is a macroprudential capital policy tool specific to Canada, applicable exclusively to D-SIBs designated by OSFI. The DSB was introduced to address the buildup of systemic vulnerabilities in the Canadian financial system — including elevated household indebtedness, housing market imbalances, and high levels of interconnectedness among large institutions — that may not be fully captured by the standard Pillar 1 capital framework.

The DSB is countercyclical in its policy design: OSFI expects to build the buffer when systemic vulnerabilities are accumulating, and to release it (fully or partially) in periods of acute stress to allow institutions to deploy their capital buffers without triggering distribution restrictions that could amplify a downturn.

### 4.2 DSB Range and Current Setting

The DSB is set within a policy range of **0% to 4.0% of total RWA**, composed entirely of CET1 capital. OSFI reviews the DSB level **semi-annually**, in June and December of each year, and publishes any adjustments with at least **one quarter's advance notice** to allow institutions adequate time to adjust their capital positions.

As of the publication date of this guideline, the DSB is set at **3.5%**, effective February 1, 2026, reflecting OSFI's assessment of currently elevated systemic vulnerabilities in the Canadian housing and household credit markets. The effective all-in CET1 requirement for D-SIBs (inclusive of all buffers) is therefore:

| Component | CET1 Requirement |
|---|---|
| Pillar 1 Minimum | 4.5% |
| Capital Conservation Buffer | 2.5% |
| D-SIB Surcharge | 1.0% |
| Domestic Stability Buffer (current) | 3.5% |
| **Total (all-in)** | **11.5%** |

### 4.3 Distribution Constraints Under the DSB

When a D-SIB's CET1 ratio falls below the sum of the Pillar 1 minimum, CCB, D-SIB surcharge, and DSB, distribution constraints are triggered. The severity of constraints escalates proportionally with the shortfall:
- **First quartile of shortfall:** Limit discretionary distributions to no more than 60% of earnings
- **Second quartile:** Limit distributions to no more than 40% of earnings
- **Third quartile:** Limit distributions to no more than 20% of earnings
- **Fourth quartile (deepest shortfall):** No discretionary distributions permitted

Institutions must submit a capital restoration plan to OSFI within **30 days** of breaching the DSB combined buffer threshold.

## 6. Applicability — Institutions Covered

### 6.1 Full Application (Large DTIs and D-SIBs)

The full set of requirements under this guideline, including all chapters and the DSB (for D-SIBs), applies to:
- The six domestic systemically important banks (D-SIBs) designated by OSFI: Bank of Montreal, Bank of Nova Scotia, Canadian Imperial Bank of Commerce, National Bank of Canada, Royal Bank of Canada, and Toronto-Dominion Bank
- All other Schedule I and Schedule II banks with total consolidated assets exceeding CAD 100 billion

### 6.2 Proportionate Application (Small and Medium-Sized Institutions)

OSFI applies proportionate expectations for smaller and less complex institutions. Institutions with total consolidated assets below CAD 100 billion may be permitted to apply simplified approaches in certain areas (e.g., the Basic Indicator Approach or simplified IRB), as set out in the relevant chapters of this guideline and in OSFI's SMI Framework communication.

### 6.3 Foreign Bank Branches

Schedule III foreign bank branches are not subject to risk-based capital ratio requirements under this guideline. Instead, they must comply with the asset-to-capital multiple and branch capital requirements set out in the FBCA Guideline. However, foreign bank subsidiaries incorporated under Schedule II are subject to the full requirements of this guideline on a solo basis.

---

## 7. Interaction with Pillar 2 and ICAAP

The CAR Guideline establishes minimum Pillar 1 capital requirements. Under Pillar 2 of the Basel framework, institutions are expected to conduct a rigorous Internal Capital Adequacy Assessment Process (ICAAP) to evaluate whether capital held is sufficient to cover all material risks — including those not fully or partially captured by Pillar 1 (e.g., concentration risk, interest rate risk in the banking book (IRRBB), pension risk, reputational risk, and strategic risk). OSFI's ICAAP guidance is set out separately in the ICAAP Supervisory Framework. OSFI may, based on its review of an institution's ICAAP, impose Pillar 2 capital add-ons above the Pillar 1 minimums.
