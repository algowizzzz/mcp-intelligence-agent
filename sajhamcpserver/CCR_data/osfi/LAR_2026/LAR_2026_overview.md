# Liquidity Adequacy Requirements (LAR) Guideline — 2026

**Issuing Authority:** Office of the Superintendent of Financial Institutions Canada (OSFI)
**Effective Date:** November 1, 2025 (applicable for fiscal years commencing in 2026)
**Replaces:** LAR Guideline — 2025 Edition
**Legislative Authority:** Bank Act, S.C. 1991, c. 46; Trust and Loan Companies Act, S.C. 1991, c. 45
**BCBS Reference:** Basel III: The Liquidity Coverage Ratio and Liquidity Risk Monitoring Tools (BCBS, January 2013); Basel III: The Net Stable Funding Ratio (BCBS, October 2014)

---

## 1. Purpose

### 1.1 Purpose and Policy Rationale

The Liquidity Adequacy Requirements (LAR) Guideline establishes OSFI's regulatory expectations for the liquidity risk management framework applicable to federally regulated deposit-taking institutions (DTIs) in Canada. The primary objectives of this guideline are to:

1. Ensure that institutions maintain sufficient high-quality liquid assets (HQLA) to survive a significant short-term liquidity stress scenario lasting 30 calendar days, without requiring external liquidity support from the central bank or government;
2. Require institutions to maintain a stable funding profile in relation to the liquidity characteristics of their assets and off-balance-sheet activities over a one-year horizon, thereby reducing the risk of an over-reliance on short-term unstable funding;
3. Promote sound liquidity risk management practices, including the maintenance of adequate liquidity buffers, diversification of funding sources, robust intraday liquidity management, and credible contingency funding planning;
4. Implement the Basel III liquidity standards as developed by the Basel Committee on Banking Supervision (BCBS), with domestic adjustments reflecting OSFI's supervisory judgment and the distinctive features of Canadian funding markets.

Liquidity risk is the risk that an institution is unable to meet its financial obligations as they fall due, or can do so only at an excessive cost that materially impairs its financial condition. Events during the 2007–2009 global financial crisis demonstrated that the rapid evaporation of market and funding liquidity could threaten the viability of individual institutions and generate systemic contagion. The LAR Guideline is designed to build structural resilience against such scenarios through minimum quantitative ratios, supervisory monitoring tools, and qualitative governance expectations.

### 1.2 Scope of Application

This guideline applies to all federally regulated DTIs, including Schedule I, II, and III banks, federally regulated trust and loan companies, and cooperative retail associations. Liquidity requirements are applied on a **fully consolidated basis** as the primary supervisory measure. OSFI may also require reporting on a solo or sub-consolidated basis for material operating entities or significant foreign subsidiaries within a banking group.

Foreign bank branches operating under Schedule III of the Bank Act are subject to OSFI's foreign bank branch liquidity guidance, which incorporates elements of this guideline adapted to the branch operating context. D-SIBs face heightened reporting requirements and may be subject to additional supervisory liquidity standards at OSFI's discretion.

## 2. Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR)

### 2.1 Overview and Minimum Requirement

The Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR) is a 30-day short-term liquidity stress metric. It measures the adequacy of an institution's stock of unencumbered high-quality liquid assets (HQLA) relative to its projected total net cash outflows over a 30-calendar-day stress scenario. The minimum LCR requirement is:

**LCR = Stock of HQLA / Total Net Cash Outflows over 30 days ≥ 100%**

An LCR of 100% or above at all times indicates that the institution holds sufficient HQLA to cover all projected net cash outflows under the prescribed stress scenario without liquidating other assets or accessing emergency central bank facilities. A breach of the 100% minimum triggers mandatory immediate notification to OSFI and submission of a written remediation plan within five business days.

### 2.2 High-Quality Liquid Assets (HQLA)

HQLA are unencumbered assets that can be converted to cash quickly, in significant volume, and with minimal loss of value in private markets during a period of liquidity stress. To qualify as HQLA, assets must meet fundamental and market-related characteristics: low credit and market risk, ease and certainty of valuation, low correlation with risky assets, listing on a developed and recognized exchange, active and sizeable market depth, and low market concentration.

HQLA are divided into Level 1 assets (the highest quality) and Level 2 assets (good quality, subject to haircuts and caps).

#### 2.2.1 Level 1 HQLA

Level 1 assets are eligible for unlimited inclusion in the HQLA stock and receive **no haircut**. They represent the most liquid and reliable assets available to an institution in a stress scenario. Level 1 assets include:

- **Cash:** Banknotes and coins, and balances on demand at the Bank of Canada and other qualifying central banks.
- **Central bank reserves:** Balances held at the Bank of Canada and other qualifying central banks of jurisdictions where the sovereign receives a **0% risk weight** under the CAR Guideline Standardized Approach, to the extent that these balances are accessible — i.e., not subject to statutory minimum reserve requirements that cannot be drawn down.
- **Marketable sovereign debt securities with 0% risk weight:** Debt securities issued or fully guaranteed by sovereigns or central banks that receive a **0% risk weight** under the CAR SA. In the Canadian context, this includes Government of Canada bonds and treasury bills, Canada Mortgage Bonds (CMBs) fully guaranteed by CMHC (a federal Crown corporation), and qualifying provincial government bonds where OSFI has approved 0% risk weight treatment under domestic policy.
- **IMF Special Drawing Rights (SDRs)** and central bank gold held as a monetary asset.

Level 1 assets constitute the core of the HQLA stock for most Canadian institutions and should represent the majority of the total buffer.

#### 2.2.2 Level 2A HQLA

Level 2A assets are subject to a minimum **15% haircut** and, together with Level 2B assets, may not exceed **40% of the total HQLA stock** (after applying all haircuts). Level 2A assets include:

- Marketable securities issued or guaranteed by sovereigns, central banks, or public sector entities (PSEs) assigned a **20% risk weight** under the CAR SA (e.g., provincial and territorial government securities of Canadian provinces not receiving 0% risk weight treatment, and qualifying MDB securities with a 20% risk weight).
- **Corporate bonds** (excluding financial sector issuers) rated **AA- or higher** by an OSFI-recognized ECAI, traded in large, deep, active liquid markets.
- **Covered bonds** (not self-issued or issued by a related entity) rated **AA- or higher**, meeting OSFI eligibility criteria.

#### 2.2.3 Level 2B HQLA

Level 2B assets are subject to higher haircuts, are capped at **15% of total HQLA** (after haircuts), and require explicit OSFI permission for inclusion. Level 2B assets include:

- **Residential mortgage-backed securities (RMBS):** Must be rated AA or higher, not self-issued, with underlying assets meeting prescribed quality criteria (prime residential mortgages, Canadian jurisdiction). Subject to a **25% haircut**.
- **Corporate bonds** (non-financial sector) rated between **A+ and BBB-**: Subject to a **50% haircut**.
- **Listed common equity shares:** Must be included in a major index (e.g., S&P/TSX Composite, S&P 500), not issued by a financial institution, and have demonstrated liquidity in a stress scenario. Subject to a **50% haircut**.

OSFI retains the discretion to restrict or reduce the eligibility of Level 2B assets for individual institutions or system-wide where prevailing market conditions suggest that prescribed haircuts do not adequately capture true liquidity risk.

### 2.3 Stressed Cash Outflows

The LCR stress scenario assumes a combined idiosyncratic and market-wide stress event lasting 30 days. Total expected cash outflows are calculated by multiplying outstanding balances or committed amounts by prescribed run-off rates:

| Liability / Facility Type | Assumed Outflow Rate |
|---|---|
| Stable retail deposits (covered by CDIC deposit insurance, low withdrawal history) | 3% |
| Less stable retail deposits (partially insured or higher withdrawal risk) | 10% |
| Operational wholesale deposits — non-financial corporates | 25% |
| Non-operational wholesale deposits — non-financial corporates | 40% |
| Non-operational wholesale deposits — financial institutions | 100% |
| Secured funding backed by Level 1 HQLA (maturing within 30 days) | 0% |
| Secured funding backed by Level 2A assets | 15% |
| Secured funding backed by Level 2B or non-HQLA collateral | 25% – 100% |
| Committed credit facilities to non-financial corporates (undrawn) | 10% |
| Committed liquidity facilities to non-financial corporates (undrawn) | 30% |
| Committed credit and liquidity facilities to financial institutions | 40% |
| Contractual derivatives outflows (net, across all derivative counterparties) | 100% of projected net outflow |
| Additional collateral outflows from rating downgrade triggers (2-notch) | 100% |
| Loss of funding from ABCP, covered bonds, ABS structures (maturing) | 100% |

### 2.4 Net Cash Outflow Calculation

Total net cash outflows are calculated as:

**Net Outflows = Total Expected Cash Outflows − min(Total Expected Cash Inflows, 75% of Total Expected Cash Outflows)**

The **75% inflow cap** prevents institutions from relying on anticipated inflows to reduce the required HQLA stock below a meaningful minimum. Even where inflows are expected to be substantial, institutions must maintain an HQLA stock equal to at least 25% of gross outflows.

## 3. Net Stable Funding Ratio (NSFR)

### 3.1 Overview and Minimum Requirement

The Net Stable Funding Ratio (NSFR) is a structural, one-year liquidity metric that requires institutions to fund their on- and off-balance-sheet activities with sufficiently stable funding sources relative to the liquidity risk profile of those assets and activities. The minimum NSFR requirement is:

**NSFR = Available Stable Funding (ASF) / Required Stable Funding (RSF) ≥ 100%**

An NSFR below 100% triggers mandatory OSFI notification and submission of a remediation plan. The NSFR complements the LCR by ensuring that institutions' structural funding profiles do not create medium- to long-term vulnerabilities, even when short-term LCR requirements are met.

### 3.2 Available Stable Funding (ASF)

ASF is the portion of an institution's capital and liabilities expected to be a reliable source of funding over a one-year horizon under both normal and stressed conditions. ASF is calculated by assigning the following ASF factors to funding categories:

| Funding Category | ASF Factor |
|---|---|
| CET1, AT1, and Tier 2 regulatory capital instruments | 100% |
| Other equity instruments and long-term liabilities with remaining maturity ≥ 1 year | 100% |
| Stable retail and small business demand and term deposits (remaining maturity < 1 year) | 95% |
| Less stable retail and small business deposits (remaining maturity < 1 year) | 90% |
| Non-financial corporate operational deposits (remaining maturity < 1 year) | 50% |
| Non-financial corporate non-operational funding (remaining maturity 6–12 months) | 50% |
| Interbank and financial institution funding (remaining maturity 6–12 months) | 30% |
| Interbank and financial institution funding (remaining maturity < 6 months) | 0% |
| All other liabilities and equity not captured above | 0% |
| Net derivative liabilities (after bilateral netting) | 0% |

### 3.3 Required Stable Funding (RSF)

RSF is the stable funding required by an institution given the liquidity profile of its assets and off-balance-sheet activities over a one-year stress horizon. RSF factors are assigned as follows:

| Asset Category | RSF Factor |
|---|---|
| Cash and central bank reserves (immediately accessible) | 0% |
| Unencumbered Level 1 HQLA (excluding cash and reserves) | 5% |
| Unencumbered Level 2A assets | 15% |
| Unencumbered Level 2B RMBS (qualifying) | 25% |
| Unencumbered Level 2B corporate bonds (A+ to BBB-) | 50% |
| Unencumbered loans to financial institutions (remaining maturity < 6 months) | 10% |
| Unencumbered loans to financial institutions (remaining maturity 6–12 months) | 15% |
| Performing residential mortgages (remaining maturity ≥ 1 year) | 65% |
| Other qualifying performing retail and SME loans (remaining maturity ≥ 1 year) | 85% |
| Performing loans to non-financial corporates (remaining maturity < 1 year) | 50% |
| Performing loans to non-financial corporates (remaining maturity ≥ 1 year) | 65% |
| Non-HQLA securities (unencumbered, not defaulted) | 50% |
| All other assets (non-performing, encumbered, or not otherwise classified) | 100% |
| Undrawn committed credit facilities extended to clients (irrevocable) | 5% |
| Undrawn committed liquidity facilities extended to clients (irrevocable) | 10% |

## 4. Intraday Liquidity

### 4.1 Intraday Liquidity Management Framework

Institutions must actively measure, monitor, and manage their intraday liquidity positions and risks to meet payment and settlement obligations on a timely basis under both normal and stressed market conditions. Key expectations include:

1. **Daily measurement of intraday liquidity usage:** Institutions must monitor peak intraday liquidity usage across all payment and settlement systems in which they participate (LVTS, ACSS, CLS, and any other relevant systems), and maintain records of daily peak usage patterns.
2. **Intraday liquidity capacity:** Institutions must maintain confirmed access to intraday liquidity — including Bank of Canada daylight overdraft facilities, pledged collateral in payment systems, and correspondent bank credit lines — sufficient to cover peak intraday outflows under normal conditions, plus a buffer for unexpected spikes.
3. **Collateral management:** Institutions must distinguish between collateral pledged for overnight secured funding and collateral available for intraday use in payment systems. Government of Canada bonds and treasury bills are the primary eligible collateral for Bank of Canada intraday facilities.
4. **Stressed intraday scenarios:** D-SIBs must demonstrate the ability to meet intraday obligations in the event of the failure of their single largest intraday counterparty or a disruption to a major payment system.

## 5. Monitoring Tools

In addition to the quantitative LCR and NSFR requirements, OSFI requires institutions to maintain and regularly report the following supervisory monitoring tools. These tools provide OSFI with a broader view of liquidity risk across time horizons and risk dimensions not fully captured by the LCR and NSFR.

### 5.1 Contractual Maturity Ladder

The contractual maturity ladder presents all contractual cash inflows and outflows from assets, liabilities, and off-balance-sheet instruments in the following prescribed time buckets: overnight, 2–7 days, 8–14 days, 15–30 days, 31–90 days, 91–180 days, 181 days to 1 year, and over 1 year. The maturity ladder identifies structural funding gaps and mismatches beyond the 30-day LCR horizon, particularly in the 3-to-12-month range, which may represent a vulnerability not directly captured by the LCR or NSFR.

### 5.2 Concentration of Funding

Institutions must report the composition of their wholesale funding base by counterparty, counterparty type, product, and currency. OSFI monitors concentration risk as a key vulnerability, since concentrated funding from a small number of counterparties or instruments can evaporate rapidly during a stress event. Required disclosures include:
- The ten largest individual wholesale funding counterparties and their share of total liabilities
- Funding by product type (unsecured deposits, repo, covered bonds, ABCP, term debt)
- Reliance on short-term (< 3 month) wholesale funding as a percentage of total funding
- Funding in each material foreign currency as a percentage of total foreign currency liabilities

### 5.3 Available Unencumbered Assets

Institutions must report the stock of available unencumbered assets that could generate liquidity in a stress scenario through either outright sale or use as collateral in secured borrowing transactions. This report distinguishes among:
- **Level 1 HQLA** already included in the LCR HQLA stock
- **Level 2 HQLA** already included in the LCR HQLA stock (2A and 2B, post-haircut)
- **Central bank eligible assets** not already counted as HQLA (e.g., certain residential mortgage loans pledged to the Bank of Canada's Standing Term Liquidity Facility)
- **Other marketable assets** that are not HQLA-eligible but may be realisable in secondary markets under stress (e.g., investment-grade bonds not meeting HQLA criteria, non-HQLA ABS)

OSFI uses this report to assess the depth of an institution's liquidity reserves beyond the primary HQLA buffer.

### 5.4 LCR by Significant Currency

Institutions with material foreign currency liabilities must calculate and report LCR positions disaggregated by significant currency (any currency representing 5% or more of total liabilities). OSFI monitors foreign currency LCR positions because, in a severe stress event, cross-currency FX swap markets may become severely disrupted, preventing institutions from freely substituting HQLA held in one currency to meet obligations denominated in another.

## 6. Reporting Requirements

### 6.1 Regulatory Reporting Frequency

Institutions must file the following liquidity returns with OSFI using prescribed templates:

| Return | Minimum Frequency | Reporting Lag |
|---|---|---|
| Liquidity Coverage Ratio (consolidated) | Monthly | 15 business days after month-end |
| LCR by Significant Currency | Monthly | 15 business days after month-end |
| Net Stable Funding Ratio (consolidated) | Quarterly | 30 business days after quarter-end |
| Contractual Maturity Ladder | Monthly | 15 business days after month-end |
| Concentration of Funding | Quarterly | 30 business days after quarter-end |
| Available Unencumbered Assets | Quarterly | 30 business days after quarter-end |

D-SIBs and institutions subject to heightened OSFI monitoring may be required to file daily LCR returns. During periods of systemic stress or heightened supervisory concern, OSFI may require any institution to file returns on an accelerated basis.

### 6.2 Public Disclosure Requirements

Pursuant to BCBS Pillar 3 disclosure standards and OSFI's Pillar 3 guidance, institutions must publicly disclose:
- Average LCR over the preceding four quarters (presented quarter-by-quarter)
- Composition of the HQLA stock by level (Level 1, Level 2A, Level 2B), with values before and after applicable haircuts
- Key drivers of LCR movements quarter-over-quarter
- Qualitative description of the institution's liquidity risk governance and intraday liquidity risk management approach

Public disclosure must be made quarterly for D-SIBs and at minimum semi-annually for all other institutions.
